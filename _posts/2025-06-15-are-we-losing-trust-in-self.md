Alongside the technical work, I write about what AI does to the humans using it. The longest piece of that thinking is an essay — *Are We Losing Trust in Self with the Advent of AI?* — and this post is a pointer to it.

The argument, compressed: AI is not the problem; it is a mirror. Its logic reflects a society already grown distrustful of ambiguity, vulnerability, and the unquantifiable. The Turing Test's true failure was never about misjudging machines — it was convincing humans that their worth lies in machinic efficiency. When we compress a person into a Gaussian-normalised feature vector and call it a representation of the human, something non-recoverable is lost in the projection.

The essay reads that predicament through an unusual pairing: Heidegger's *enframing* — technology ordering people into a "standing reserve" awaiting optimisation — alongside Ibn Arabi's *Wahdat al-Wujud*, in which nothing that exists, techne included, is foreign to its source. Byung-Chul Han's burnout society explains what the daily leak of self-trust feels like; Rumi's field beyond right and wrong marks the territory no model trained on our labels can enter. It closes with a concept I keep building on: **Organic Species Intelligence** — mind, body, and soul as one intelligence, and the hierarchy any responsible system should assume: OSI > AI.
